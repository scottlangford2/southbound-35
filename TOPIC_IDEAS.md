# Blog Topic Ideas

Candidate topics for future Southbound 35 posts, anchored to the blog's
focus on public finance and economic development along the Texas I-35
corridor, with occasional sports and religion essays.

The economic-development section below is built from a re-reading of the
two posts in that lane: "The Hays County Growth Story" (2026-04-06) and
"Where Is All of This Going?" (2026-04-13).

---

## Themes already present in the economic-development posts

1. **Growth as a corridor story, not a county story.** Both posts
   frame Hays against its neighbors (Travis, Williamson) and against
   Texas history (Williamson's 2010 population as Hays's 2060
   benchmark). The unit of analysis is the I-35 corridor, not any
   single jurisdiction.
2. **Affordability as the causal engine.** The $135K Travis-to-Hays
   median-price gap is presented as the mechanism driving in-migration,
   not just a correlate of it.
3. **Municipal unbundling.** County totals hide wildly different
   trajectories at the city level (Kyle's 150%+ decadal growth vs.
   Wimberley's near-flat line). Disaggregation is a recurring move.
4. **Forecast uncertainty treated seriously.** The projections post
   shows six methods, labels them honestly (R², migration
   assumptions), and refuses to pick one. That willingness to sit with
   a 460K–1.4M range is a brand-defining stance.
5. **Physical and institutional constraints bind the forecast.**
   Water studies, voided road bonds, and planning capacity are named
   as the things that could break the extrapolations. Growth is
   conditional, not given.
6. **Triangulation across official sources.** Census decennial, ACS,
   PEP via FRED, TDC Vintage 2024, CAMPO 2045 RTP, Redfin, ABoR. The
   methodological stance is "show your sources and reconcile them."
7. **Residential-price and rooftops framing.** Both posts lean on
   housing and population. Non-residential base — jobs, sales, ag,
   industrial — appears only through the Kyle warehouse aside.

## Additional themes worth developing

8. **Who arrives and who leaves.** IRS SOI county-to-county migration
   flows and ACS origin data can put faces on the net-migration number
   the projections treat as a scalar.
9. **Where the arrivals work.** LEHD LODES commute flows would test
   how much of Hays is a bedroom county for Austin vs. building its
   own job base — the unresolved question the CAMPO forecast assumes
   away.
10. **The non-residential tax base.** Property-tax rolls by class
    (residential / commercial / industrial / ag / personal) across
    corridor cities would show who's converting rooftops into a
    balanced fiscal base and who isn't.
11. **Cost of service vs. revenue at the margin.** New single-family
    rooftops typically cost more in services than they pay in
    property tax — a standard result from the AFT/COCS literature
    that hasn't been tested on Hays.
12. **Special districts and overlapping jurisdictions.** MUDs, WCIDs,
    ESDs, PIDs, MMDs — the invisible layer of local government where
    much of the growth-era debt actually sits.
13. **Local debt capacity and ratings.** G.O. vs. revenue debt,
    I&S rates, rating-agency outlooks. Bond-market signals are an
    external check on the planner narrative.
14. **School-district capital programs.** Hays CISD and Dripping
    Springs ISD bond elections are where growth meets voters, and
    where the residential-property-tax bill actually lands.
15. **Land-use and entitlement velocity.** Permits issued, plats
    approved, COs granted. Leading indicators that sit upstream of
    Census counts.
16. **Historical analogs.** Frisco/McKinney (1990–2010),
    Round Rock/Pflugerville (1980–2000), Conroe/Magnolia
    (2010–2020). What did their growth curves do after year 20?
17. **The corridor as a string of links.** Temple–Waco–Austin–
    San Marcos–San Antonio–Laredo as an economic chain, each link
    with its own base industry and fiscal profile.
18. **Small business formation and entrepreneurship.** Business
    Formation Statistics (BFS) high-propensity applications by
    corridor county — ties directly to the author's JBV Insights
    work.
19. **Workforce pipeline.** Texas State and ACC award-to-employer
    flows; how much of the local labor market is locally educated.
20. **Megaproject finance on I-35 itself.** TxDOT Capital Express
    Central/North/South, SH 130 debt restructuring, and who pays
    for corridor capacity.

## Topic ideas by theme

### Theme 1–3: Corridor framing, affordability, municipal unbundling

- **Five cities on one interstate.** Round Rock, Pflugerville,
  Kyle, New Braunfels, Schertz — ranked on growth, median price,
  sales-tax per capita, debt per capita. A single comparative table
  as the anchor.
- **The $135K gap, five years later.** Replicate the affordability
  figure quarterly from 2020 to present; ask whether the gap is
  closing, stable, or widening and what that implies for Hays's
  migration pipeline.
- **Buda and Dripping Springs as the next Kyle.** What the
  2010-to-2025 Kyle curve predicts for the two cities one growth
  cycle behind it.

### Theme 4–5: Forecast uncertainty and binding constraints

- **The voided $440M Hays County road bond.** What the Tex. Elec.
  Code §4.003 notice defect actually was, and the base rate at
  which Texas bond elections get reversed on procedural grounds.
- **Hays County's first comprehensive water study since 2011.**
  Edwards Aquifer, rule of capture, Region K planning, and what
  612K residents imply for groundwater. Natural pair with the
  projections post.
- **What breaks the logistic fit.** A sensitivity post: which
  assumption (migration rate, carrying capacity, price gap) has
  to move how far to collapse the 612K mid-scenario to the 460K
  low.

### Theme 6: Methodological / data posts

- **Reading the TDC Vintage 2024 release.** An explainer on how
  the migration scenarios are constructed and why the 0.5 scenario
  is the one most outside agencies adopt.
- **FRED vs. PEP vs. ACS for small-area population.** Which
  series to trust for which question, with a worked Hays example.

### Theme 7 + 10–11: Non-residential base and fiscal balance

- **Kyle's warehouse economy.** Is Central Texas logistics a
  durable tax base or a low-wage filler? Property-tax-per-acre of
  distribution vs. retail vs. residential, using Hays CAD roll
  data.
- **The rooftops-vs.-rooftops-plus-jobs question.** A cost-of-
  community-services study for one Hays city, following the AFT
  methodology.
- **Sales-tax per capita on the corridor.** San Marcos (outlet-
  mall anchored) vs. Kyle (rooftop-heavy) vs. New Braunfels
  (tourism-heavy). What the mix means for fiscal resilience.

### Theme 8–9: Migration flows and commuting

- **Who moves to Hays County?** IRS SOI county-to-county flows
  2015–2023, mapped. Which metros feed the in-migration, and how
  income of in-migrants compares to stayers.
- **The Austin doughnut.** Travis's flat core vs. Hays /
  Williamson / Bastrop / Caldwell rings, tested against the
  monocentric-city model.
- **Where Hays residents actually work.** LEHD LODES commute
  origin-destination pairs; share of workers commuting to Travis,
  Comal, Bexar, and staying in-county over time.

### Theme 12–14: The invisible local-government layer

- **MUDs in Hays and Comal.** Governance, debt loads, and what
  happens on annexation.
- **TIRZs and 380 agreements between New Braunfels and Belton.**
  Who's using them and where the incidence falls.
- **Hays CISD as growth fiscal policy.** Enrollment curves, bond
  programs, debt service, and the golden-penny question.

### Theme 15: Leading indicators

- **Entitlement velocity in Kyle and Buda.** Monthly permit and
  plat data as a leading indicator of the next ACS release.

### Theme 16: Historical analogs

- **What did Williamson County do after it hit 302K?** A
  retrospective on 2005–2020 Williamson — jobs, sales tax, debt,
  schools — as a template for Hays's next fifteen years.
- **Frisco/McKinney as the upper bound.** How a Dallas exurb pair
  built a non-residential base and what that implies for the
  Hays-cities' options.

### Theme 17–20: The rest of the corridor

- **Temple as the next inflection point.** Baylor Scott & White,
  the Amazon fulfillment center, and whether Temple is running
  Hays's 2005 playbook.
- **SH 130 vs. I-35.** The toll road's second life as a freight
  bypass and what that means for I-35 expansion politics.
- **San Marcos as a college town's balance sheet.** Texas State
  as anchor institution; compare to Denton/UNT and
  College Station/A&M.
- **High-propensity business formations on the corridor.**
  BFS applications per capita, county by county, 2018–present.
- **Data centers and ERCOT load growth on I-35.** Siting,
  transmission, and who pays for grid upgrades.
